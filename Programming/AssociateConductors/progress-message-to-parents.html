Progress Message To Parents

. Vision

As the season progresses, and we move toward the performances, directors of each ensemble send an update to the parents called a “Progress Message,” letting them know how the season has been going, what unique and exciting things have thus far been accomplished, and what is important for them to focus on during the final weeks ahead.

. Procedures

Please compose your Progress Message according to the following guidelines—

  • Greeting
    • Dear Parents of the Minuet Level Los Angeles,
    • Dear Parents of the Sonata Level New York,
    • Dear Parents of the Prelude Level Washington, D.C.,
  • Two Paragraphs — Please compose this yourself.
    • Paragraph (1): Create opening sentences on how well the season has been going, with specifics on what has been accomplished. You can also include a few of the joyful and fun aspects of this season in particular.
    • Paragraph (2): Talk about, build up and generate excitement over the upcoming concert, and make sure to reiterate the day, date, time and location of the event. Encourage parents to invite family and friends to the concert, and ask them to click on the button below to secure their tickets right away.
  • Concert Schedule — NCC will do this section for you.
    • We will draw attention to the remaining rehearsals of the season, and give another opportunity for parents to download and review the PDF of the Season Schedule. (this section may be standardized for each Progress Message, and populated by Dr. Blackstone; so no need to compose yourself).
  • NCC Uniform — NCC will do this section for you.
    • We will include a section with uniform protocol, ensuring that each student is in possession of all uniform pieces, and is clear on the NCC’s grooming requirements. We will also provide the link once again reiterating the instructions on how to order the uniform. (this section may be standardized for each Progress Message, and populated by Dr. Blackstone; so no need to compose yourself).
  • Proofing
    • As listed above, the main area for you to compose yourself is the “Two Paragraphs” section listed above. The other sections will be completed for you by the NCC. Before submitting your “Two Paragraphs,” be sure you have proofed your message carefully according to the guidelines below.
    • Spelling
      • Spelling errors are symptomatic of a lack of professionalism, and always reflect poorly on an organization. Every word must be correctly spelled.
      • Check each word by eye individually for spelling accuracy, reading the piece both forwards and backwards.
      • If you are ever unsure about the spelling or syntax of any word you see, look it up and double-check.
    • Grammar and Punctuation
      • Grammatical errors are symptomatic of a lack of professionalism, and always reflect poorly on an organization. Grammar and punctuation must be correct.
    • Vocabulary
      • Word choice is very important because words are impactful and influential. Therefore, special attention must be paid to the words used when we communicate on behalf of the NCC.
      • Descriptive words should be exciting, displaying creativity and intelligence. They should not, however, be inaccessible or cause confusion to the average person. Any word that the average person would need to look up in a dictionary should be avoided, as it can come across as disconnected, out of touch, or pretentious. The goal is to create beautiful, compelling statements while still remaining 100% accessible.
    • Repeated Words
      • Some words must be repeated in a writing piece, such as “the” or “and.” Descriptive words, however, should not be repeated within a single writing piece, whenever possible, such as “wonderful” or “amazing.” When the same descriptive word is used over and over, it does not come across as resourceful or creative, especially when an author has the entire English language to work with.
      • Any time a descriptive word is used, make sure to scan the entire writing piece to make sure it did not get repeated.
      • When you find a word that has been repeated, use your knowledge or a thesaurus to find alternate words to use instead.
    • Buzz Words/Phrases
      • Certain words are encouraged to be used when writing about/for the NCC, because we do want them associated with our brand.
      • Below is a list of words that are in alignment with our brand.
        • “Education”/“educational”
        • “Journey”/“trajectory”/“process”/“growth”
        • “Extraordinary”
        • “Joy”/“Joyful”
        • “Discipline”
        • “Structure”
        • “Leadership”
        • “Artistry”
        • “Creativity”
    • Banned Words/Phrases
      • Certain words are banned in any copy published by the NCC, because we do not want them associated with our brand.
      • Below is a list, with possible substitutions to use instead.
        • “Kid”/“kids” — Too casual for the NCC. Use “child”/“children”/“singer”/ “vocalist”/“student”/“chorister”.
        • “Unfortunately” — This word is disappointing and negative. Say “as it turns out”, or rephrase the information so that it doesn’t sound “unfortunate”.
        • “Change” as in “the schedule for this week has been changed” — People don’t like change and the word can be alarming. Say “adjusted” or rephrase the sentence to sound less dramatic: “We have updated the uniform to include a black tie, which will enhance the professional image of our students” as opposed to “the uniform has been changed.” Every piece of information we deliver must be presented in the most positive light possible. The exact same information can come across in very different ways, depending on how it is said.
    • Phrasing
      • Sometimes, words in a sentence must be put into a slightly different order so that the writing piece reads as smoothly as possible. Be aware of awkward sentence structure, and make sure to correct it whenever it doesn’t feel quite right.
    • Flow
      • Each sentence should flow naturally into the next. Be aware of abrupt changes of subject, and correct whenever something doesn’t feel like it naturally follows. Make sure all transitions are smooth. Here is an example of random sentences that do not flow well together, and sound very scattered: “This season, the students will be working on head tone. Our 2nd piece in the concert is a piece of music by Bach. Remember to go over the schedule carefully.” Here is the same information, with smoother transitions: “This season, the students will be working on head tone placement. This new technique will resound beautifully in our December concert, especially on the 2nd piece of the program which was composed by J.S. Bach. As we progress over the next few months towards this exciting holiday event, we ask that you review the schedule regularly to ensure the most successful result.”
    • Nomenclature
        • It’s very important to use the correct NCC names for things in order to prevent confusion on the part of the members, as well as provide a consistent branding within our organization. Below is a list of official NCC names with samples of how they are sometimes mistakenly said.
          • Premier Ensemble (not Premier Group, Premier Singers, Premier Chorus)
          • Debut Ensemble
          • Concerto Level (not Concerto singers, Concerto voices, Concerto Choir)
          • Sonata Level
          • Minuet Level
          • Prelude Level
          • National Children’s Chorus (not National Children’s Choir, National Children’s Chorale)

. Results

If the Progress Message is done well, it will elicit a sense of delight, satisfaction and excitement in the parent who is reading it, which, beyond making him or her happy, could potentially lead to increased ticket sales and greater desire to donate to the NCC, further supporting our educational programs. As you can see, creating the right dynamic is very important in expressing the culture of our brand, and has direct implications to our bottom line (as does every interaction we have with our members). The Progress Message is a great opportunity for you, our Associate Conductors, to connect with our parents and share the extraordinary things you are creating with their children in the classroom each and every week.

If the Progress Message is not done well, you will notice that many edits, changes and re-writing must take place, going back and forth, usually at the expense of your time, as well as the time of our management team. This is something ideally to be avoided. If the directions above are followed carefully, edits to the Progress Message should be minimal or unnecessary.
