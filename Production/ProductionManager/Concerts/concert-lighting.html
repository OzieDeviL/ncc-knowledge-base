Concert Lighting

Maximizing the experience of music

. Vision

The concert is an experience of heightened senses. When a person commits to being an audience member, he or she is committing, whether consciously or unconsciously, to exist for a limited time in a state of enhanced focus and sensory processing. It is the job of the concert producer to understand this and to create an experience that effectively considers and cares for the senses of the audience member at every moment during the event. The concert producer is the ultimate event planner, and must make sure that everything the guest processes is thoughtfully planned to a point which allows complete surrender. This means people will experience soaring through beauty without ever being brought crashing down by a glitch, error or unpleasant feeling caused by lack of consideration for their experience.

The concert is measured against itself. It is a separate world from the real world. The producer must strive to create contrast within the event so that all choices matter and have an impact. Nothing should get old or lose its freshness.

. Procedures

General Uses of Lighting

Lighting should be used to enhance the impact of the music. We use the visual sense to sharpen the auditory sense. Lighting should never draw attention to itself. The goal is for every audience member to walk away thinking, “Wow, the music sounded beautiful.” We never want audience members to actually comment on the lighting. The visuals must always serve the music and occupy a place of secondary importance in the consciousness of the audience.

Extremely bright white “concert lighting” is rarely beautiful or effective. Orchestras, especially, benefit from light with some amber tones that is not at 100% strength, balanced carefully with the ability of the musicians to read their scores. This “fireside lighting” allows the patina of the instruments to glow, and the audience can actually see the orchestra better. If the orchestra is lit this way, so that the yellow/gold tones of the instruments are highlighted, then blue is a great choice for a background, since blue is amber’s complimentary color on the color wheel.

There are some colors that register very strongly in the human consciousness and must be used sparingly. Pure Red is an example of this. Pure Red should be used with great discretion, and only briefly for very dramatic effects. It generally reads “anger” or “hell” or “lust.” Don’t overuse Pure Red. One appearance per concert is generally the maximum.

For some songs which are more pop or modern, it can be effective to create a concept which includes a distinct shape or image. As with using Pure Red, one should only apply this one time in the concert. Otherwise, it loses its effect. They will remember specific looks and immediately be bored by seeing them repeated.

How to Select Lighting Colors

Red — Hell, anger, high drama

Magenta — love, sweetness, warmth, lack of conflict

Orange — optimistic, exotic, Asian, “Pop”

Yellow — happy, sunshine, major key, bright, hopeful

Turquoise — soothing, peaceful, neutral (Note: Pure Kelly Green is a very strong color and should be used as sparingly as Pure Red. A Pure Kelly Green background has been used only once in 10 years of NCC performances, on the piece “A Girl’s Garden.”)

Blue — quiet, lullaby, nighttime, reverent, holy, mystical, chord clusters

Purple — strong, ambitious, sexy, bold
