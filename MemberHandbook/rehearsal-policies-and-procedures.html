. New York Drop-off

In order to ensure the safety of our students, the NCC requires strict adherence to the procedure below. All persons authorized to pick up your child must read and comply with this document. If you have any questions or require further clarification, please contact our office.

Procedures

  • Upon arrival, parents are asked to enter the church office, and proceed immediately to Room 101 (first door on the right). Please do not wait or congregate in the lobby.
  • Approach the Check-in Desk with the NCC sign, where NCC Authorized Personnel will document the Check-in, and point your child in the right direction. Please do not wait or congregate in Room 101—Check-in must be fast and efficient. Parents are not permitted within the limits of the secured area.
  • As soon as your child is checked-in, kindly vacate the building as quickly as possible, in order to avoid congestion in Room 101, and allow other students checking in to have the space they need to do so. Please do not wait around or congregate in Room 101 or the lobby. Because of the small amount of space, and the large number of students, parents are not permitted to stay on-site at any point during the rehearsal process.
  • Check-in procedures at Room 101 will conclude at precisely 4:27 PM, at which time the room must be completely vacated, except for instructors and students. At this point, the door to Room 101 shall be closed, instructors and students shall take their places for rehearsal and all other check-ins shall be handled from the entrance to the lobby. Again, please do not congregate in the lobby, or anywhere on-site. As soon as your child is checked-in, please quickly vacate the building to avoid congestion within the small amount of space we have to accomplish these large-scale operations.
  • For all check-ins at 4:27 PM or later, please approach NCC Authorized Personnel in the lobby, where he or she will document your child’s check-in, while another Parent Chaperone escorts your child to his or her rehearsal room.

Procedures

. Attendance

Being Present…

At all levels of the NCC (Prelude through Scholars), our instructors teach new and varied material at each session, and at a fast pace. Accordingly, even one missed session results in a significant loss of learning. Through years of experience, we have observed that when attendance dips below 80%, unacceptable signs of unpreparedness typically begin to manifest and students are no longer able perform at the professional level that is expected of our organization. As a result, it is imperative that a strict code of attendance and punctuality be observed.

Absences

Two (2) absences are allowed per season (Fall, Winter or Spring). The third absence will require advance permission from the ensemble’s director. Unexcused absences beyond two (2) may result in non-participation in ensuing concert events.

Tardies

Students are to be in place five (5) minutes before the start time of any session, and must stay for its entirety. As latecomers miss important material and disrupt the rehearsal underway, we ask that punctuality be made a top priority for each NCC family. Since arriving late is deemed highly unprofessional and unacceptable in the real world of working musicians, please make sure to support your children’s learning of what it truly takes to succeed by getting them there on time. They will one day thank you for a lesson well-learned—one that will benefit them for the rest of their lives.

Half-Absences and Quarter-Absences

Any tardy or early dismissal which causes the student to miss more than fifteen (15) minutes of rehearsal time is counted as one half of an absence.

Any tardy or early dismissal which causes a student to miss less than fifteen (15) minutes of rehearsal time is counted as a quarter of an absence.

Half-Absences and Quarter-Absences will add up and be counted towards a student’s maximum allowance of two (2) absences per season, which cannot be exceeded as written above.

. Tech Rehearsals

Putting on the Finishing Touches

At the end of each season, just prior to any major concert, the students at the NCC embark on an intensive musical journey, where the most detailed and rigorous work of the season is completed. During this time, which we call “Tech”, students practice at an accelerated rate and take their already excellent product to a level of true professional refinement.

Mandatory Attendance

Absences, tardy arrivals and early dismissals are not allowed for any Tech Rehearsal. Directors can and will pull students out of any event where they are deemed “unprepared.” As all Tech Rehearsals are indicated on the schedule months in advance, we urge you to adjust your schedules accordingly to make sure nothing is missed.

Wise Planning

We know how busy the students’ schedules are. From homework, to sports, to other extra-curricular activities, there are many demands on their time. Therefore, we do our absolute best throughout the year to create a regimen that is consistent, easy and that works most harmoniously with the students’ lives. Tech Rehearsals, however, comprise the one period prior to any major performance where we need the students’ schedules to bend to the needs of the ensemble, and lend time additionally in the pursuit of refinement and polish.

For this reason, we ask that you plan well in advance of any Tech Rehearsals marked on the schedule, clearing out the students’ calendars of other activities, and minimizing their work to just school and the NCC. If this means postponing parties, events and outings to a different time, then please do so. We need their time during Tech Rehearsals, and we also need your cooperation in making sure they get ample rest for the performance, which usually means trimming down other activities.

We know how busy you, the parents, are as well. As much as this organization exists for the benefit of the students, we wish the NCC experience to be hassle-free and enjoyable for you too. Therefore, we urge you to take the time to carefully go through the schedule, making sure there are no conflicts with your family calendars, and that all planning issues are dealt with as far in advance as possible. Helping you avoid last-minute emergencies and stressful situations is important to us, so please take our thoughtful advice and plan ahead.

. Rehearsal Expectations & Behavioral Standards

Being Your Best: Proper Singing Posture

Students will be expected to uphold proper posture when singing in order to facilitate excellent breath support—

  • Feet shoulder width apart.
  • One foot slightly in front of the other.
  • Back up straight.
  • Music chest high.
  • All of the above for sitting, plus back away from the back of the chair, sitting on the upper 3 rd of the seat.

Being You Best: General Guidelines

  1. Students must have a positive attitude—
    • Willingness to work.
    • Encouraging others.
    • Answering questions.
    • Taking risks.
  2. Students must not talk while in rehearsal, except by permission of the director—
    • Raise hand to ask a question or to make a comment.
    • Raise index finger to indicate a bathroom use request.
    • Only ask questions in front of the group that concern the entire group—private matters are to be discussed with the director alone.
    • Never express negativity in front of the group.
  3. Students must use the bathroom facilities before rehearsal, after rehearsal or during the break. Use of the bathroom during the practice itself is for emergencies only.
  4. Students must behave respectfully and appropriately to the director and their peers at all times. The following offenses are grounds for suspension and/or permanent dismissal from the program—
    • Talking back to an instructor.
    • Disrespectful sarcasm to an instructor.
    • Inappropriate language.
    • Racial slurs.
    • Social intimidation.
    • Threats.
    • Intentional physical injury to another.
    • Defiance against any member of the NCC Artistic and Administrative Team.
  5. Students must remember to bring their materials to practice—
      • NCC Music Folder and Pencil.
      • Scholars, Los Angeles.
      • Premier Ensemble, Los Angeles and New York.
      • Debut Ensemble, Los Angeles and New York.
    • NCC Binders.
      • Sonata Level, Los Angeles and New York.
      • Minuet Level, Los Angeles and New York.
    • Other possible materials needed (advance notice will always be given).
      • Kodly books.
      • Theory books.
      • Batons.
      • Conducting books.

Policy

Electronics are not allowed at NCC sessions, except for mobile phones which must remain off and stowed away (out of sight) for the full duration of the session (including breaks). Students are not to play with mobile phones or make calls/texts until rehearsal is over. They are to be used for emergency situations only.

Doing What is Best for Your Children…

  • There is not a large amount of downtime, so there is simply no need to have electronic entertainment.
  • During short breaks or waiting time prior to rehearsal, students are encouraged to engage in social interaction (not bury and isolate themselves in an electronic device).
  • Not all students have the devices, so it is inappropriate for some to be using them while others cannot.
  • Music, community and teamwork are the focal points of our rehearsals; therefore, unrelated devices that promote an antisocial environment are counterproductive to our mission and cannot be allowed.

. Observing Rehearsal

Creating a Productive Space

Imagine that you are working efficiently on a project alone in a room. Everything required for your project is in the room, and you are in a place where you can create freely with no fear of judgment. About an hour into your work, a friend and someone you don’t know unexpectedly enters the room, and wishes to watch you at work. Though the same work exists in front of you, something has definitely changed. Instead of your entire consciousness focusing freely on the work itself, you are now intently aware that you are being watched and you begin to cater your work to meet the satisfaction of those watching, thereby somewhat inhibiting the free creative space that existed before they entered. You are a professional, and very good at what you do, so you can handle this and still do a good job. But on some level, you know that your work could have been better if your friend and other person weren’t there.

This is exactly the type of thing that happens when people come to observe the NCC at work. Our directors and students are skilled enough to do a good job under any social circumstance, but it cannot be denied by either directors or students that observation prior to the final product inhibits the freedom to make mistakes, try new things and take the chance of looking or sounding bad—the very thing that ultimately leads an artist or ensemble to discovering the very best and most brilliant work they have to offer.

For this reason, rehearsals at the NCC are “closed.” This means that other than our Artistic Team and students, visitors are not allowed as a general rule. Exceptions must always be cleared in advance with the ensemble’s director who must be given the opportunity to strategize for the temporary loss of artistic freedom as described above. If you have an important reason to view an NCC rehearsal, or if you have someone in mind that you feel needs to see our process, contact the group’s director who, depending on the situation, may or may not be able to accommodate your request.

. Supporting the Conductor’s Work

Protocol for Performance Days

All of us at the NCC—students, parents, and Artistic Team members—want the best performance possible. It is what we strive for and work towards at every rehearsal week to week. Accordingly, we place all of our focus on the student performers, making sure they have what they need in order to deliver the finest performance possible. But there is another performer whose musical job the night of the concert is of tremendous difficulty and extreme importance—the conductor. With so much emphasis placed on the performers who are actually singing or playing, the role of the conductor can sometimes be taken for granted or entirely forgotten. And yet if a conductor were to miss a single cue, or fail to properly indicate any given musical entrance, the outcome would be disastrous—in fact, a piece of music might have to be completely stopped and restarted as a result, in front of the audience. Now consider that in a given concert, a conductor will have to encounter upwards of five hundred similar instances, all with the same crucial degree of importance. We could call this “multi-tasking at its ultimate level,” otherwise known as the job of musical conducting.

By the time a conductor reaches the final moments prior to a performance, he or she is swimming among countless musical details, mentally going over them time and time again. At this point, the level of concentration and focus required to succeed is extraordinary, and takes every ounce of effort and energy.

Knowing and understanding what is going on with the conductor just prior to a performance suddenly changes one’s perspective, and immediately makes it obvious to observe the following courteous protocol the day of the performance so that the conductor is allowed to be “in the zone” and not distracted:

  • Do not call the conductor.
  • Do not text the conductor.
  • Do not e-mail the conductor.
  • Do not approach the conductor or engage him or her in any conversation beyond “Hello,” should you see him or her prior to the performance in the parking lot, concert hall lobby, backstage, artist entrance, or anywhere in passing.
  • If there is an emergency, please contact the Executive Director or Executive Administrator, who will pass the information along to the conductor if necessary and appropriate.

Protocol for Rehearsal Days

Now, here is something that may come as a surprise. A musical rehearsal, even a weekly rehearsal, takes almost as much focus and concentration on the part of the conductor as a performance. Our directors are very ambitious, and tremendously efficient, which means that each rehearsal is planned down to the minute, and must be executed to perfection if superb concert preparation over the weeks is to be accomplished. Again, knowing what is going on with the conductor just prior to any rehearsal immediately makes it obvious to observe the following courteous protocol the day of a rehearsal:

  • Do not call the conductor.
  • Do not text the conductor.
  • E-mailing the conductor is OK on a rehearsal day, but a response may not happen until the following day.
  • Do not approach the conductor or engage him or her in any conversation beyond “Hello,” should you see him or her prior to the rehearsal.
  • If there is an emergency, please contact the Executive Administrator, who will pass the information along to the conductor if necessary and appropriate.

The Appropriate Time

Ok, I understand. But if I can’t approach the director before or after any rehearsal or performance, does that mean he or she is never available to speak with me about my issue?

No, to the contrary. Our directors are extremely available to discuss any and all issues you feel are important to you. Simply send an e-mail to the director with whom you wish to meet, request to schedule an appointment, and an appropriate time will be set to give you and your child the level of attention you require. Either by phone, or in person, our directors are always happy to be of service in the best possible way.
