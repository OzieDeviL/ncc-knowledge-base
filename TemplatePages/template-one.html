Creating the space for greatness

. Vision

The concert is an experience of heightened senses. When a person commits to being an audience member, he or she is committing, whether consciously or unconsciously, to exist for a limited time in a state of enhanced focus and sensory processing. It is the job of the concert producer to understand this and to create an experience that effectively considers and cares for the senses of the audience member at every moment during the event. The concert producer is the ultimate event planner, and must make sure that everything the guest processes is thoughtfully planned to a point which allows complete surrender. This means people will experience soaring through beauty without ever being brought crashing down by a glitch, error or unpleasant feeling caused by lack of consideration for their experience.

The concert is measured against itself. It is a separate world from the real world. The producer must strive to create contrast within the event so that all choices matter and have an impact. Nothing should get old or lose its freshness.

General Uses of Lighting

Lighting should be used to enhance the impact of the music. We use the visual sense to sharpen the auditory sense. Lighting should never draw attention to itself. The goal is for every audience member to walk away thinking, “Wow, the music sounded beautiful.” We never want audience members to actually comment on the lighting. The visuals must always serve the music and occupy a place of secondary importance in the consciousness of the audience.

Extremely bright white “concert lighting” is rarely beautiful or effective. Orchestras, especially, benefit from light with some amber tones that is not at 100% strength, balanced carefully with the ability of the musicians to read their scores. This “fireside lighting” allows the patina of the instruments to glow, and the audience can actually see the orchestra better. If the orchestra is lit this way, so that the yellow/gold tones of the instruments are highlighted, then blue is a great choice for a background, since blue is amber’s complimentary color on the color wheel.

There are some colors that register very strongly in the human consciousness and must be used sparingly. Pure Red is an example of this. Pure Red should be used with great discretion, and only briefly for very dramatic effects. It generally reads “anger” or “hell” or “lust.” Don’t overuse Pure Red. One appearance per concert is generally the maximum.

For some songs which are more pop or modern, it can be effective to create a concept which includes a distinct shape or image. As with using Pure Red, one should only apply this one time in the concert. Otherwise, it loses its effect. The example on the left was created for “Love Train,” and the look only appeared on that single song in the performance. Audience members’ memory lasts the entire performance. They will remember specific looks and immediately be bored by seeing them repeated.

How to Select Lighting Colors

Red — Hell, anger, high drama

Magenta — love, sweetness, warmth, lack of conflict

Orange — optimistic, exotic, Asian, “Pop”

Yellow — happy, sunshine, major key, bright, hopeful

Turquoise — soothing, peaceful, neutral (Note: Pure Kelly Green is a very strong color and should be used as sparingly as Pure Red. A Pure Kelly Green background has been used only once in 10 years of NCC performances, on the piece “A Girl’s Garden.”)

Blue — quiet, lullaby, nighttime, reverent, holy, mystical, chord clusters

Purple — strong, ambitious, sexy, bold

Musical Guideline

Your musical set should consist of:

  • At least one (1) foreign language piece.
  • Varied tempos, keys, colors, moods, styles.
  • Classical, folk, modern, jazz or Broadway selections.
  • A vocal range appropriate for teaching head voice in the bel canto style.
  • The vocal range should not extend below middle C (C4) and should include notes up through high F or G (F5 or G5).
  • If a piece you are interested in sites too low in the register, please find a higher arrangement or have it transposed to a higher key.

Number of Pieces per NCC Ensemble

  • Prelude Level - three (3) selections per season.
  • Minuet Level - three (3) to four (4) selections per season.
  • Sonata Level - four (4) selections per season.
  • Concerto Level - four (4) to five (5) selections.

    • Spelling errors are symptomatic of a lack of professionalism, and always reflect poorly on an organization. Every word must be correctly spelled.
    • Check each word by eye individually for spelling accuracy, reading the piece both forwards and backwards.
    • When reading forwards, make sure spelling is correct not only individually, but in context of what is being said. For instance, “peak” individually is spelled correctly, but it is incorrectly spelled when talking of a “sneak peek.” The same thing applies to “premier” versus “premiere.”
    • When reading backwards, start at the end and check each word by itself. This will often help you catch errors your eye did not see when reading forwards.
      • Make sure to run a computer spellcheck for good measure.
    • Be careful of "auto-correct," which can change correctly-spelled words to incorrect, and be sure to verify the final PDF or final version of anything prior to approval.
    • If you are ever unsure about the spelling or syntax of any word you see, look it up and double-check.
  • Grammar and Punctuation
    • Grammatical errors are symptomatic of a lack of professionalism, and always reflect poorly on an organization. Grammar and punctuation must be correct, except in unique circumstances when an exception is made on purpose for stylistic reasons.

. Results

Signs that indicate the rehearsal is being executed well:

  • Rehearsal moves at a fast and efficient pace, with an accelerated mode of learning for the students.
  • Conductor does minimal talking to the pianist, and nearly 100% of the conductor's focus is on the chorus.
  • The piano playing sounds beautiful, polished, and serves to inspire the students' love and learning of the piece.
  • Mistakes are rare, and it feels like the pianist is in sync with the conductor.

Signs that indicate the rehearsal is being executed well:

  • Rehearsal is continually disrupted, because conductor is having to give too much/frequent instruction to the pianist.
  • Pitches are not being given in a timely manner, or in the manner prescribed.
  • Pianist is not following the path of the rehearsal and frequently is unaware of where the conductor is starting, causing disruptions and delays, breaking the flow of the rehearsal and slowing down its productivity.
  • Pianist does not know how the music should go; seems like the pianist has never heard the piece; pianist has not prepared the score; pianist is leaving out a lot of the notes written in the music; pianist is not accurately representing how the score should sound; pianist is making many errors and playing wrong notes, which is confusing the students.
  • Pianist and conductor are not in sync; pianist is not following the beat.
